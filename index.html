Preface

Political Science is the scientific study of power and governance. Many non-social scientists often think of Political Science as philosophy, as debating the current political issues and campaigns. However, this couldn’t be further from the truth.

Political Science today is a very quantitative and mathematical discipline: open any Political Science Academic Journal, and you could mistake it for an applied statistics or mathematics journal. Political scientists today frequently use mathematical models to create causal hypotheses, and advanced statistical/econometric techniques to prove our causal hypotheses.

This handbook is a guide to Political Science, focusing on modern quantitative methodology and its applications to Political Science Research. I take a very “political-economy” based approach - using more quantitative tools from the discipline of economics, to study political phenomena.

This handbook is divided into sections, which are further subdivided into modules, which are further subdivided into chapters. The first three sections focus on Political Science methods and how they work. The 4th section focuses on a variety of topics currently discussed in political science.

  • The final 5th section, on mathematics, is meant to be a supplementary section: It is not essential to know all the concepts in this section, but, it may be useful to reference to better understand the methods being used.

These sections do not have to be done in order, with the exception that it is highly recommended to do some of section 1 (modules 1 & 2) before section 2. You also do not need to complete all the modules in a section - each section takes you to a postgraduate level, which may be beyond what is needed for your interests.


Section 1: Statistical Methods

Statistics is the backbone of modern Political Science and Political Economy. Why is this the case? In political science, we are often interested in relationships between variables. How does democracy affect economic growth? How do different voting systems affect women representation?Statistical tools allow us to describe the real world, and establish causal relationships.

This is a full course in statistics/econometrics, experimental design, and causal inference for Political Science. Section 1 introduces basic statistical concepts, while Section 2 and 3 discuss the most common regression models used in Political Science research. Section 4 introduces ways in which we can establish causality in our research. Sections 5 and 6 are advanced (rarely covered in undergraduate programmes), but help deal with some more advanced topics in political science.

The modules are meant to be learned in order, so later modules assume knowledge on earlier modules. Other prerequisites and requirements are laid out in the respective modules. The course primarily uses the R-programming language to create models. Some math background from Mathematics for Political Science would be very useful, especially up to the end of Module 3.

Section 2: Applied Machine Learning

In recent decades, Political Science has increasingly adopted machine learning techniques. These more advanced and complex techniques, while reducing the ability to interpret the results, allow us to make quite accurate predictions. Machine learning has also enabled the conversion of text-based data into numerical numbers, allowing us to conduct statistical analyses on text data.

This course is about machine learning techniques employed in the social sciences. The modules are meant to be learned in order, so later modules assume knowledge on earlier modules. This course assumes knowledge up to the end of module 3 of Statistical Methods. Other prerequisites and requirements are laid out in the respective modules. The course primarily uses the R-programming language. Some math background from Mathematics for Political Science would be very useful, especially up to the end of Module 3.

Module Chapters
1 Non-Linear Prediction Models Polynomial Models, Step Functions and Splines, Local Linear Regression, Generalised Additive Models, Bagging and Random Forest
2 Classification Methods Naive Bayes Classifier, Tree-Based Classifier, Evaluation of Classification Models
3 Model Selection and Validation Training and Testing Sets, K-Fold Cross Validation, Bootstrap Sampling, Regularisation
4 Multivariate Dimensional Reduction Principle Components Analysis, Factor Analysis, Cluster Analysis
5 Text Mining Text Analysis Basics, Sentiment Analysis, Similarity Metrics, Text-Based Classification, Topic Models


Section 3: Game Theory and Mathematical Modelling

Game Theory is the mathematical modelling of political situations and decision making. Why do we want to model political situations? Well, the world is very, very complex, especially when we are studying human behaviour as in the social sciences. Thus, models simplify reality, and focus on only some features of reality that we care about. These models can provide insights for scholars in the social sciences, and help decision makers.

This is a full course in Game Theory for Political Science. Thus, we focus on the political science applications of game theory, while de-emphasising mathematical proofs, and avoiding concepts that are not applicable to Political Science.

The modules are meant to be learned in order, so later modules assume knowledge on earlier modules. Other prerequisites and requirements are laid out in the respective modules. Some probability background is useful, so module 1 of Statistical Methods is recommended as a pre-requisite. Some math background from Mathematics for Political Science would be very useful, especially up to module 3.

Module Chapters
1 Static Games Introduction to Game Theory, Nash Equilibrium and Static Games, Spatial Models, Mixed Strategies
2 Dynamic Games Dynamic Games of Complete Information, Imperfect and Incomplete Information, Bayesian Games
3 Advanced Games Nim and Combinatorial Games, Bimatrix Games

Section 4: Topics in Political Science

Of course, having a strong base in methodology is very important - how else are you going to understand research papers? However, methodology is only a tool - a tool to help us understand and explore phenomena in Political Science.

This section has a series of modules on different aspects of Political Science. There is no pre-requisites to this course - even someone with minimal methodological training can learn much from these modules. However, a strong understanding of methodology will be very useful in understanding how the conclusions presented in this module were obtained.


Section 5: Mathematics for Political Science

This section is mostly a supplemental section to the other modules. This section contains most of the mathematics that are needed for the methods utilised in this handbook. If you need a refresher on any of the mathematics involved, this is the place to reference.

While it may be possible to learn mathematics from this section, this isn’t the goal of this section. There is no practice problems, and practice is essential for learning maths. This section mainly is a refresher for concepts relevant to Political Science.

Resources for Learning Statistics and R-Programming

  • For those who need an introduction to statistics, I recommend the Khan Academy course on AP Stats for a mathematical introduction: https://www.khanacademy.org/math/ap-statistics

  • There is also a terrific free textbook on Statistical learning with applications to R: https://www.statlearning.com

  • BurkeyAcademy provides free online econometrics courses and implementations in R: https://www.burkeyacademy.com/statistics-econometrics

  • For more advanced individuals who want to learn more in-depth about statistics, see this excellent MIT lecture series: https://www.youtube.com/playlist?list=PLUl4u3cNGP61ATaGTFcSp7bhogloD2wHP

  • A free resource to learn R with is CodeAcademy: https://www.codecademy.com/enrolled/courses/learn-r

  • For those who have spare cash to spend, there is an excellent R programming course (Data Analysis with R) offered by IBM on Coursera, which teaches R and statistics: https://www.coursera.org/learn/data-analysis-with-r

  • I also highly recommend those who can afford it, to attend the London School of Economic (LSE) Summer School, which has an excellent course - ME314 Introduction to Data Science and Machine Learning. Many of these methods are covered in depth in this course, and the course is run by one of the developers of Quanteda, the main textual-analysis R package.
